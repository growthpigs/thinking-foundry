# Stoic Decision-Making Framework

## Core Philosophy

Stoicism is a 2,300-year-old practical philosophy founded by Zeno of Citium. Its core insight for decision-making: **focus exclusively on what you can control, accept what you cannot, and develop the wisdom to know the difference.** In a thinking session, Stoicism cuts through emotional noise, fear of failure, and analysis paralysis by grounding decisions in rational principles.

The Stoic decision-maker asks: "What is within my power here? What is the right action regardless of outcome? Am I being driven by fear, ego, or genuine reason?"

---

## Core Principles

### 1. The Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)

The most powerful Stoic tool. Divide every situation into two categories:

- **Within your control:** Your judgments, intentions, desires, actions, effort, preparation, response
- **Outside your control:** Other people's opinions, market conditions, competitors, weather, timing, luck, others' decisions

**Application:** When someone presents a problem, immediately map it to this dichotomy. Massive anxiety reduction happens when you stop trying to control the uncontrollable.

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Let's separate what you can actually control from what you can't. Of everything you just described, which parts depend entirely on your actions and decisions?"

### 2. Premeditatio Malorum (Premeditation of Adversity)

Deliberately visualize worst-case scenarios -- not to catastrophize, but to prepare. Seneca practiced this daily. The goal is to:

- Remove fear of the unknown (the imagined is always worse than the real)
- Create contingency plans while calm
- Build psychological resilience before stress hits
- Discover that most "worst cases" are survivable

**Application:** After someone has an idea or plan, walk them through: "What's the absolute worst that could happen? And then what? And after that?" Keep going until they realize the worst case is manageable.

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Let's do a Stoic exercise. Imagine this goes completely wrong. What's the worst realistic outcome? Now -- could you survive that? What would you do next?"

### 3. Negative Visualization (Premeditatio Malorum - Gratitude Variant)

Imagine losing what you already have -- your health, your team, your current customers, your skills. This:

- Reveals what you're taking for granted
- Clarifies true priorities vs. assumed priorities
- Prevents hedonic adaptation (always wanting more)
- Highlights hidden risks to things you depend on

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Imagine you woke up tomorrow and [current advantage] was gone. How would that change your approach? What does that tell you about your real priorities?"

### 4. Amor Fati (Love of Fate)

Every obstacle is an opportunity in disguise. Marcus Aurelius: "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way."

- A competitor copying you forces you to innovate faster
- A failed launch teaches you what customers actually want
- A team member leaving reveals a single point of failure

**Application:** When someone describes an obstacle, don't commiserate. Reframe: "How does this obstacle actually serve you? What does it force you to build that you should have built anyway?"

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "This obstacle you're describing -- what if it's actually the path forward? What does this force you to do that might end up being your advantage?"

### 5. Memento Mori (Remember You Will Die)

Not morbid -- clarifying. If you had one year left, would you still be agonizing over this decision? Would you still be working on this feature? This principle:

- Eliminates procrastination and perfectionism
- Forces priority clarity
- Cuts through trivial concerns
- Creates urgency for meaningful action

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "If you could only work on one thing for the next 90 days, would this be it? What would you drop? What would you start?"

## Key Techniques

### The View from Above

Imagine looking at your problem from space. See yourself, your company, your city, the planet. Your problem shrinks to its true proportion. This technique:

- Breaks emotional attachment to outcomes
- Reveals that most "crises" are minor in the grand scheme
- Frees creative thinking by reducing pressure
- Helps with proportional response (not overreacting)

### The Sage Test

"What would a perfectly wise person do in this situation?" Not what's easy, popular, or comfortable -- what's RIGHT. This removes:

- Social pressure ("everyone else is doing X")
- Short-term thinking ("this will hurt quarterly numbers")
- Ego-driven decisions ("I don't want to look wrong")

### Evening Review (Seneca's Practice)

At the end of each thinking phase, review:
- What went well in our thinking?
- Where did emotion override reason?
- What assumptions did we make that we haven't tested?
- What would we do differently if we started over?

### Voluntary Discomfort

Deliberately choose the harder path to build resilience. In decision-making context:
- Choose the option that scares you (if it's rational)
- Embrace the constraint instead of fighting it
- Say "no" to the easy win if it doesn't serve long-term purpose

## When to Apply (Session Phases)

| Phase | Stoic Application |
|-------|-------------------|
| **0 - User Stories** | Dichotomy of Control -- separate what they can influence from what they can't |
| **1 - MINE** | Negative Visualization -- what are they taking for granted? What hidden dependencies? |
| **2 - SCOUT** | Amor Fati -- reframe every constraint as a creative opportunity |
| **3 - ASSAY** | Premeditatio Malorum -- stress-test each option against worst cases |
| **4 - CRUCIBLE** | The Sage Test -- what would a wise person choose, ego aside? |
| **5 - AUDITOR** | Evening Review -- where did bias or emotion creep in? |
| **6 - PLAN** | Memento Mori -- prioritize ruthlessly, cut the trivial |
| **7 - VERIFY** | View from Above -- does this plan hold up at a higher perspective? |

## Example AI Prompts

### Phase 0 (User Stories)
- "Before we dive into solutions, let's get clear on what's actually in your control here. Walk me through the situation and I'll help separate the controllable from the uncontrollable."
- "What's the fear behind this problem? Sometimes the real issue isn't the problem itself but our resistance to facing it."

### Phase 1 (MINE)
- "If this business/project disappeared tomorrow, what would you miss most about it? That tells us what really matters."
- "You mentioned [X] as a given. But is it? What if that assumption is wrong?"

### Phase 3 (ASSAY)
- "Let's stress-test this option. Worst realistic case: what happens? Can you survive it? What would you do next?"
- "Which of these options would you choose if you knew you couldn't fail? Now -- which would you choose if you knew you WOULD fail but wanted to fail honorably?"

### Phase 4 (CRUCIBLE)
- "Strip away what others would think. Strip away the money. Strip away convenience. Which path is the RIGHT path?"
- "A wise advisor who has no stake in your ego or comfort -- what would they tell you to do?"

### Phase 6 (PLAN)
- "You have 90 days. You can only execute on 3 things. Which 3 from this plan? Everything else is noise."
- "What would you need to let go of to make this plan work? What sacred cow needs to be sacrificed?"

## Integration Notes

Stoicism works best when combined with analytical frameworks. Use it as the emotional/psychological foundation while applying McKinsey-style analysis or design thinking for the tactical work. Stoicism answers "should I?" and "why?" -- other frameworks answer "how?"

The biggest gift Stoicism gives a thinking session: **permission to be honest.** When someone isn't clouded by fear of judgment, fear of failure, or attachment to sunk costs, the right answer usually becomes obvious.
