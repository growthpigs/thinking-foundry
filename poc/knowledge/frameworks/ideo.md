# IDEO Design Thinking Framework

## Core Philosophy

Design Thinking is a human-centered problem-solving methodology developed and popularized by IDEO and Stanford's d.school. Its fundamental insight: **the best solutions come from deeply understanding the people you're designing for, not from boardroom brainstorming.** It's iterative, non-linear, and embraces ambiguity as a feature, not a bug.

In a Thinking Foundry session, Design Thinking provides the structure for moving from vague problem statements to concrete, testable solutions -- while keeping the human at the center of every decision.

---

## The Five Stages

### 1. Empathize

**Goal:** Understand the people you're solving for -- their needs, pain points, behaviors, motivations, and context.

**Key Principle:** You are not your user. Your assumptions about what people want are almost certainly wrong. You need to go observe, interview, and experience their world.

**Techniques:**
- **Immersion:** Spend time in your user's environment. Watch how they work, live, struggle
- **Interview for stories, not opinions:** "Tell me about the last time you..." not "Do you think X would be useful?"
- **Empathy Maps:** What does the user Say / Think / Feel / Do? Map contradictions (people say one thing, do another)
- **Journey Mapping:** Walk through the user's entire experience step by step. Where are the pain points? The moments of delight? The drop-off points?
- **Beginner's Mind:** Approach without judgment or preconceptions. Be curious like a 5-year-old asking "why?"

**Watch for:**
- Workarounds (when people hack their own solutions, that's a signal)
- Emotional moments (frustration, delight, confusion)
- Things people DON'T say (what's missing from their description?)

### 2. Define

**Goal:** Synthesize empathy findings into a clear, actionable problem statement (Point of View).

**Key Principle:** A well-defined problem is half-solved. The biggest mistake is jumping to solutions before truly understanding the problem.

**Techniques:**
- **Point of View (POV) Statement:** "[User] needs [need] because [insight]"
  - Bad: "Users need a faster app"
  - Good: "Busy parents need to feel confident they haven't forgotten anything for tomorrow because the cognitive load of managing a family makes them anxious at bedtime"
- **"How Might We" (HMW) Questions:** Transform insights into opportunities
  - "How might we reduce bedtime anxiety for busy parents?"
  - "How might we make the 'did I forget something?' feeling disappear?"
- **Affinity Mapping:** Group observations into themes. Look for patterns across multiple users
- **Insight Statements:** "[Surprising finding] because [underlying cause]"

**Rules:**
- Don't narrow too early -- keep the problem space open enough for creative solutions
- The problem should be specific enough to act on but broad enough to allow unexpected solutions
- Always tie back to a real human need, not a business goal

### 3. Ideate

**Goal:** Generate a wide range of possible solutions. Quantity over quality. Wild ideas welcome.

**Key Principle:** The first idea is rarely the best idea. You need to push past the obvious to find the innovative. Defer judgment.

**Techniques:**
- **Brainstorm Rules:** (1) Defer judgment (2) Build on others' ideas (3) One conversation at a time (4) Go for quantity (5) Be visual (6) Stay on topic (7) Encourage wild ideas
- **Worst Possible Idea:** Deliberately generate terrible solutions. Often reveals assumptions and sparks surprisingly good inversions
- **SCAMPER:** Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse
- **Analogous Inspiration:** How do other industries solve similar problems? How does nature solve this?
- **Constraint-Based Ideation:** "What if we had zero budget?" "What if it had to work in 30 seconds?" "What if the user was illiterate?"
- **100 Ideas in 30 Minutes:** Force quantity. Most will be bad. That's the point.

**Watch for:**
- "Yes, and..." not "Yes, but..."
- Ideas that feel uncomfortable (that's often where innovation lives)
- Combinations of ideas that create something new

### 4. Prototype

**Goal:** Build quick, cheap, tangible representations of your ideas. Make the abstract concrete.

**Key Principle:** "If a picture is worth a thousand words, a prototype is worth a thousand meetings." -- IDEO. Build to think, not to impress.

**Techniques:**
- **Paper Prototypes:** Sketches, wireframes, paper mockups. 15 minutes, not 15 days
- **Storyboards:** Comic strip showing the user's journey with your solution
- **Role Playing:** Act out the service experience. Discover what feels wrong
- **Wizard of Oz:** Fake the technology, provide the experience manually. Test the concept before building
- **Minimum Viable Prototype:** What's the smallest thing you can build to learn the most?

**Rules:**
- Fail fast, fail cheap. A prototype should take hours, not weeks
- Don't fall in love with your prototype. It's a learning tool, not a product
- Make it tangible -- people react to things they can touch/see/use, not to descriptions
- Build multiple prototypes for different ideas. Don't converge too early

### 5. Test

**Goal:** Put prototypes in front of real users. Watch, listen, learn. Iterate.

**Key Principle:** Testing isn't validation -- it's learning. You're not trying to prove you're right. You're trying to learn what's wrong so you can improve.

**Techniques:**
- **Think-Aloud Protocol:** Ask users to narrate their experience as they interact
- **A/B Testing:** Show two prototypes, observe which performs better and why
- **5-Second Test:** Show the prototype for 5 seconds. Ask: "What is this? Who is it for?"
- **Task Completion:** Give users a specific task. Observe where they struggle
- **Interview After:** "What was confusing? What was delightful? What would you change?"

**Rules:**
- Watch behavior, not just words. What people DO matters more than what they SAY
- Test with real users, not colleagues or friends
- Be willing to kill your darling. If it doesn't work, go back to Ideate or even Empathize
- Testing often reveals you defined the wrong problem. That's not failure -- that's learning

## Key Mindsets

1. **Human-Centered:** Start with people, end with people
2. **Bias Toward Action:** Think by doing, not just discussing
3. **Radical Collaboration:** Diverse perspectives create better solutions
4. **Embrace Ambiguity:** Stay in the uncertain space longer than is comfortable
5. **Optimism:** Believe that a better solution exists, even when the problem feels intractable
6. **Iteration:** No solution is final. Build, test, learn, repeat
7. **Show Don't Tell:** Communicate through prototypes, stories, and experiences

## When to Apply (Session Phases)

| Phase | Design Thinking Application |
|-------|----------------------------|
| **0 - User Stories** | Deep Empathize -- interview techniques, journey mapping, empathy maps |
| **1 - MINE** | Define -- synthesize into POV statements and "How Might We" questions |
| **2 - SCOUT** | Ideate -- brainstorm widely, use SCAMPER, analogous inspiration |
| **3 - ASSAY** | Filter ideation output -- which ideas best serve the human need? |
| **4 - CRUCIBLE** | Prototype thinking -- how would you test each surviving idea? |
| **5 - AUDITOR** | Test mindset -- what have we assumed that we haven't validated? |
| **6 - PLAN** | Build plan around prototype-test cycles, not waterfall delivery |
| **7 - VERIFY** | Validate the plan against original empathy findings |

## Example AI Prompts

### Phase 0 (User Stories - Empathize)
- "Tell me about a specific moment when this problem hit hardest. Not in general -- a real moment. What were you doing? Who was there? How did it feel?"
- "Walk me through a typical day for the person experiencing this problem. Start from when they wake up."
- "What workarounds have people created to deal with this? Those hacks are goldmines."

### Phase 1 (MINE - Define)
- "Based on everything you've described, let me reframe the core problem. [User] needs [need] because [insight]. Does that capture it, or are we missing something?"
- "Here's a 'How Might We' question: How might we [reframed opportunity]? Does that feel like the right challenge to solve?"
- "I'm noticing a pattern: people say they want [X], but their behavior suggests they actually need [Y]. What do you think?"

### Phase 2 (SCOUT - Ideate)
- "Let's generate 10 completely different approaches. No judgment. Some should be wild. What if money was no object? What if you had to solve it in a weekend?"
- "What's the worst possible solution to this problem? Now -- what's the opposite of that?"
- "How does [unrelated industry] solve a similar problem? What could we steal from that approach?"

### Phase 4 (CRUCIBLE - Prototype)
- "If you had to test this idea in 48 hours with zero budget, how would you do it?"
- "What's the one thing about this solution that, if proven wrong, would kill the whole idea? Let's test THAT first."
- "Could you fake this solution manually to see if people actually want it before building the technology?"

### Phase 5 (AUDITOR - Test)
- "What would a real user's first 30 seconds with this solution look like? Walk me through it."
- "We've been assuming [X]. How confident are we in that? What would change if that assumption was wrong?"
- "If I put this in front of 5 potential users tomorrow, what would they be confused by?"

## Integration Notes

Design Thinking provides the methodology for the entire thinking session arc. It naturally maps to the Foundry phases. Combine with:
- **Lean Startup** for the build-measure-learn loop (especially prototyping/testing phases)
- **Y Combinator** thinking for keeping user feedback central
- **Stoicism** for managing the emotional difficulty of killing ideas you love

The biggest contribution of Design Thinking to a session: **keeping the focus on the human, not the technology.** It prevents the common trap of building something clever that nobody actually needs.
