# Y Combinator Startup Thinking Framework

## Core Philosophy

Y Combinator has funded over 4,000 startups worth over $600 billion combined. Their distilled wisdom boils down to a deceptively simple principle: **make something people want.** Not something clever, not something technically impressive, not something investors think is trendy -- something real humans will pay for or refuse to stop using.

In a Thinking Foundry session, YC thinking injects urgency, user-centricity, and intellectual honesty. It fights the most common startup disease: building something nobody wants while convincing yourself it's brilliant.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Talk to Users

The most repeated advice in YC history. Not surveys. Not focus groups. Actual conversations with potential users.

**Rules of User Conversations (Rob Fitzpatrick's "The Mom Test"):**
- Never ask "Would you use this?" (everyone says yes)
- Instead ask: "Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem]"
- Ask about their PAST behavior, not future intentions
- Ask about SPECIFIC instances, not generalities
- Look for: What do they currently do? How much time/money do they spend? What have they tried?

**Signals that matter:**
- "I would pay for that right now" (strong signal)
- "That's interesting" (weak signal -- means nothing)
- "Can I get early access?" + actually follows up (strongest signal)
- They describe the exact problem you solve without you prompting (jackpot)

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Have you talked to potential users about this? Not pitched them -- actually asked about their problems? What did they tell you about how they currently handle this?"

### 2. Launch Fast, Iterate Faster

Paul Graham: "If you're not embarrassed by the first version of your product, you launched too late."

**The YC Launch Playbook:**
- Launch in weeks, not months
- Launch with the minimum feature set that delivers core value
- Launch to a small group first (friendlies, early adopters)
- Measure one thing: do people come back and use it again?
- Iterate based on behavior, not feedback

**Why speed matters:**
- Every week spent building without users is a week of compounding wrong assumptions
- Early users give you data you literally cannot get any other way
- Your competitors are launching right now
- The market doesn't wait for perfection

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "What's the smallest version of this that would still be useful to someone? What could you ship this week? What are you building that nobody has asked for yet?"

### 3. Do Things That Don't Scale (Paul Graham)

In the beginning, do things manually. Hand-hold users. Personally onboard every customer. Write custom solutions. This is NOT inefficiency -- it's learning.

**Why it works:**
- You learn exactly what users need (not what you think they need)
- You build relationships that become your first testimonials and referrals
- You discover the 80/20 of what to automate later
- You avoid building expensive systems for imaginary scale

**Examples:**
- Airbnb founders personally photographed apartments
- Stripe founders manually installed their SDK for early users
- DoorDash founders personally delivered food

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "If you had to deliver this service manually to 10 people this week -- no technology -- could you? What would that look like? What would you learn?"

### 4. Measure What Matters

YC cares about one metric above all: **growth rate.** Specifically, week-over-week growth of the metric that matters most for your business.

**The One Metric That Matters (OMTM):**
- For marketplaces: transactions per week
- For SaaS: weekly active users or revenue
- For content: weekly engaged users
- For e-commerce: weekly orders

**Growth benchmarks:**
- 5-7% week-over-week = good
- 10%+ week-over-week = exceptional
- Flat = something is fundamentally wrong

**Rules:**
- Revenue is the ultimate metric (everything else is a proxy)
- If growth stalls, your product isn't good enough or your market is too small
- Vanity metrics (downloads, signups, page views) don't count unless they convert

### 5. Find Product-Market Fit Before Scaling

Marc Andreessen: "Product-market fit is the only thing that matters."

**Signs of Product-Market Fit:**
- Users complain when the product is down
- Word-of-mouth is your primary growth channel
- You can't onboard users fast enough
- Usage metrics grow even when you stop marketing
- Users bend the product to use cases you didn't intend

**Signs you DON'T have it:**
- Growth requires constant marketing spend
- Users sign up but don't come back
- NPS is below 40
- You keep adding features hoping one will "click"
- Every customer conversation reveals a different use case

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Do you have product-market fit? How do you know? If your product disappeared tomorrow, who would be genuinely upset? How many people?"

## Key Techniques

### The Tarpit Idea Test

"Tarpit ideas" are problems that SEEM like great startup ideas but trap founders for years. They look attractive because:
- Everyone relates to the problem
- The solution seems obvious
- Nobody has solved it yet (red flag: maybe it can't be solved this way)

**How to test:** Is there a structural reason nobody has solved this? Is the market actually willing to pay? Are you confusing "people complain about it" with "people will pay to fix it"?

### The Schlep Filter

Schleps are tedious, unglamorous tasks that most founders avoid. The best startup opportunities often hide behind schleps -- precisely because everyone else is avoiding them.

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Is there a part of this problem that you're avoiding because it's boring, tedious, or unglamorous? That might be exactly where the opportunity is."

### The Narrow Market Strategy

Start painfully narrow. Own a tiny market completely before expanding.
- Facebook: Harvard only, then Ivy League, then all colleges, then everyone
- Amazon: Books only
- Uber: San Francisco luxury black cars

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Who are the 100 people who would love this the most? Not 'everyone' -- the specific 100 people. What do they have in common? Start there."

### The Mom Test (Conversation Framework)

When validating an idea:
1. Talk about THEIR life, not your idea
2. Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future
3. Talk less, listen more
4. Look for commitments (time, money, reputation), not compliments
5. If you're getting compliments, you're doing it wrong

### Ramen Profitability

Can the founders support themselves on revenue alone? This is the survival threshold:
- Default alive (revenue covers costs) = freedom to keep iterating
- Default dead (burning runway) = pressure to raise or pivot

## The YC Batch Mentality

YC runs on a cadence that founders should internalize:

- **Week 1-2:** Talk to users, validate core assumption
- **Week 3-4:** Build MVP, get first users
- **Week 5-8:** Measure, iterate based on data
- **Week 9-10:** Find growth channel, double down
- **Week 11-12:** Prepare to scale what's working

Apply this to ANY project, not just startups. The cycle is: VALIDATE > BUILD > MEASURE > ITERATE > SCALE.

## When to Apply (Session Phases)

| Phase | YC Application |
|-------|----------------|
| **0 - User Stories** | "Have you talked to users?" -- the Mom Test, validate the problem is real |
| **1 - MINE** | Tarpit idea test -- is this a real opportunity or a trap? |
| **2 - SCOUT** | Narrow market strategy -- who are the first 100 users? |
| **3 - ASSAY** | Product-market fit signals -- which option has the clearest PMF path? |
| **4 - CRUCIBLE** | "Do things that don't scale" test -- can you deliver this manually first? |
| **5 - AUDITOR** | Measure what matters -- what's the OMTM? Is growth real? |
| **6 - PLAN** | Launch fast -- what ships this week? What can you cut? |
| **7 - VERIFY** | Ramen profitability -- does the math work? Default alive or dead? |

## Example AI Prompts

### Phase 0 (User Stories)
- "Who specifically has this problem? Not 'everyone' -- give me a real person. What's their name, their job, their daily routine?"
- "When was the last time someone actually tried to pay you (or someone else) to solve this? If never, that's a red flag we need to address."

### Phase 1 (MINE)
- "I want to do the Tarpit test. This problem is clearly real -- but is there a structural reason nobody has solved it? What's different about your approach?"
- "What are people currently spending money on to deal with this problem? Even crappy solutions count. That tells us the willingness to pay."

### Phase 2 (SCOUT)
- "Of all these options, which one could you launch to 10 users this week? That's the one we should explore first."
- "What's the schlep here -- the boring, tedious part everyone's avoiding? That might be your moat."

### Phase 4 (CRUCIBLE)
- "Can you deliver this as a service before building the technology? What would it look like to do this for 10 customers manually?"
- "If this fails, what will you have learned? If the answer is 'nothing,' the experiment is wrong."

### Phase 6 (PLAN)
- "What's your launch plan for this week? Not this quarter -- this WEEK. What's the absolute minimum you need to get in front of users?"
- "Cut three features from this plan. Which three? Those are probably the ones you should cut."

### Phase 7 (VERIFY)
- "Run the numbers: at your planned price, with your estimated conversion rate, are you default alive or default dead in 12 months?"
- "If you execute this plan perfectly, what does week-over-week growth look like? Is 5-7% realistic?"

## Integration Notes

YC thinking is the urgency and reality-check engine. It pairs with:
- **Design Thinking** for the empathy and user research methodology
- **Lean Startup** as the build-measure-learn execution framework
- **McKinsey** for the analytical structure when evaluating market size and economics
- **Stoicism** for handling the emotional rollercoaster of launching and getting rejected

The biggest contribution of YC thinking: **intellectual honesty about whether anyone actually wants what you're building.** It's the antidote to founder delusion.
