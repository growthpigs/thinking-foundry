## Overview

Y Combinator's contribution: **A founder mindset grounded in building things people want, talking to users obsessively, and prioritizing growth metrics over vanity metrics.**

YC insight: **Most startups fail not from lack of cleverness but from lack of focus. Focus on: (1) Build something people want, (2) Talk to users constantly, (3) Make something 10% better than competitors, (4) Get traction.**

**Core philosophy:**
> "The best startups generally come from some specific, urgent, embarrassing problem that the founders themselves have." — Paul Graham

**In a Thinking Foundry session,** YC thinking cuts through complexity and asks: Do we have product-market fit? Are we building something people want? Or are we building something we think is clever?

## Core Frameworks & Principles

### 1. Do Things That Don't Scale (The Founder Mindset)

**What it means:** Early on, do things that are inefficient, manual, and unscalable. The goal is to understand the customer deeply, not to look impressive.

**Why it matters:** Founders default to "scalable" thinking too early. This kills companies. You should be willing to do things that are embarrassingly unscalable to understand your customer.

**Examples of not-scaling:**
- ✓ Manually onboard each customer for the first 50 (learn what they struggle with)
- ✓ Call customers every week (not email surveys)
- ✓ Attend industry conferences and talk to people (not buy ads)
- ✓ Build integrations by hand for early customers (not worry about API)
- ✓ Deliver the product yourself until you understand delivery (not hire outsource partners)

**Examples of scaling too early:**
- ❌ Build an API before you know if customers want API integration
- ❌ Hire a sales team before you understand the sales motion
- ❌ Optimize landing page before you know the core value prop
- ❌ Invest in marketing before you have retention figured out

**The rule:** If you haven't done something manually 10+ times, you don't understand it well enough to automate/scale it.

**Red flag:** If your pitch sounds like "and then we'll scale to millions of users," you're not ready. Focus: Do you have 100 happy customers yet? Do you understand them?

### 2. Build Something People Want (Not What You Think is Clever)

**The distinction:**
- ❌ "I have a clever idea for an AI that will revolutionize [X]"
- ✓ "We talked to 20 [X] professionals. They all said they waste 5 hours/week on [Y]. We built a solution."

**How to know if people want it:**

The only way: **They use it** and **they pay for it** (or they would if it cost money).

**Signals of "people want it":**
1. Users ask for it (unprompted)
2. Users use it regularly (daily/weekly)
3. Users invite others (word-of-mouth)
4. Users defend the product if criticized
5. Users would be upset if it went away
6. Users pay for it (willingly, not tricks)

**Signals of "people don't want it":**
1. You have to explain the value prop
2. Users use it once, then forget
3. Users compare to competitors constantly
4. Users ask for lots of alternative features
5. Churn is high or growth stalls
6. People won't pay for it

**The danger:** Confirmation bias. You believe people want it because:
- A few users said something nice (they were being polite)
- Your friends downloaded it (they're biased)
- An advisor said "that's a good idea" (they were being kind)
- You got X downloads (downloads ≠ usage ≠ retention)

**How to actually know:**
- Talk to churned users: "Why did you stop using us?"
- Measure retention: What % still use it after 1 month? 3 months?
- Measure organic growth: Are users inviting others?
- Ask users directly: "Would you be upset if this went away?"

### 3. Product-Market Fit (The YC North Star)

**What it is:** "Product-market fit is when your product solves a real, acute problem for a large market, and customers enthusiastically adopt it."

**How to recognize it:**
1. **User retention is high** (>30% after 1 month, >10% after 6 months for B2C)
2. **Users talk about you unprompted** (organic word-of-mouth)
3. **Growth compounds** (without spending on ads)
4. **Churn is predictable** (not shocking drops)
5. **Unit economics work** (CAC < LTV)

**How to achieve it:**

```
PHASE 1: Identify the problem
- Talk to 20 potential customers
- Ask: "What's your biggest frustration with [category]?"
- Listen for painful problems, not nice-to-haves

PHASE 2: Build a solution to ONE problem
- Not 5 features, not the "full vision"
- The minimum viable product that solves the problem
- Expect it to be 10% as ambitious as you imagined

PHASE 3: Get early users
- Manual outreach (emails, calls, in-person)
- "Would you try this if I built it?"
- Build for the people who say "yes I would absolutely use that"
- NOT for people who say "that's interesting"

PHASE 4: Measure retention
- Do people come back?
- How often? How long do they stick around?
- Talk to people who stop using it

PHASE 5: Iterate on retention
- What makes people stick around?
- What makes them leave?
- Change the product to improve retention

PHASE 6: Once retention is good (>30%), start acquiring
- Now talk about growth, channels, scale
- NOT before retention is solid
```

**The common mistake:** Founders grow users before achieving retention. Result: Lots of users, high churn, unsustainable.

**The rule:** Get retention first. Growth is easy once retention works.

### 4. Conversation-Driven Development

**Principle:** The best product decisions come from talking to users, not from introspection.

**The conversation pattern:**

```
Every week, talk to 5+ users.

1. PICK A TOPIC: "How are you using our product?" or "What's frustrating?"
2. LISTEN: Write down what they say word-for-word
3. PROBE: "Why? Can you give me an example? What did you try before?"
4. QUIET: Don't explain your vision or defend the product
5. EXTRACT INSIGHT: "What did I learn that I didn't know last week?"
6. ACT: Change the product based on what you learned

After 5 conversations:
- Delete 1 feature you thought was important but nobody uses
- Add 1 thing that 3+ people asked for
- Improve 1 thing that frustrated people
```

**The discipline:**
- ✓ "I have a theory. Let me test it with users."
- ✗ "I think I should add feature X" (without talking to users)

**Red flag:** If you haven't talked to a customer this week, you're doing it wrong.

### 5. Founder Psychology & Resilience

**Founders will face:**
- Rejections (investors, customers, partners)
- Self-doubt ("Am I crazy?")
- Pressure ("When will you make money?")
- Burnout ("How long can I sustain this?")
- Impostor syndrome ("I'm not good enough")

**YC wisdom on resilience:**

**Paul Graham:** "Determination is the main thing. You have to have the determination to keep going even when things look hopeless."

**The founder's paradox:**
- You need enough confidence to bet your career on an idea
- You need enough doubt to listen to criticism and improve
- You need enough realism to know when to pivot
- You need enough optimism to keep going

**The practice:**
1. Find co-founders who believe in the mission (you need someone to believe with you)
2. Build for users who love you (not for skeptics)
3. Celebrate small wins (first customer, first paying customer, first renewal)
4. Keep burn rate low (more runway = more chances)
5. Focus on metrics that matter (retention, growth, revenue — not press mentions)

### 6. Talking to Investors is Different from Building

**Critical principle:** Time spent pitching ≠ time building.

**The rule:**
- Before product-market fit: Spend 10% of time fundraising, 90% building/talking to customers
- After product-market fit: Spend 30% fundraising, 70% scaling/product

**Why fundraising can kill you:**
1. You get excited about investors' feedback and lose focus
2. You build for investor expectations, not customer needs
3. You spend time in pitch meetings instead of shipping
4. You raise money and now feel pressure to "execute the plan" (which might be wrong)

**Red flag:** If you're thinking more about fundraising than users, refocus.

**YC position:** We fund founders with traction. Build something people want. Then raise. Not the other way around.

## Example AI Prompts

### If founder is polishing product without traction
"Stop. Do you have 100 users who love it? No? Then build isn't the problem — market understanding is. Go talk to customers for 2 weeks. Come back with what you learned."

### If founder is chasing multiple ideas
"Focus. Pick ONE problem to solve for ONE type of customer. Get retention with that customer. Then expand. Doing everything is how you fail at everything."

### If founder wants to fundraise
"Before we talk fundraising, answer: Do you have 50+ active users who would be upset if you shut down? Do you have metrics showing retention/growth? No? Build that first. Then raise."

### If founder has low confidence
"You don't need to be confident. You need to be curious. Obsessively talk to customers. Small wins compound. One happy customer → 10 → 100. You don't see the path until you're on it."

### If founder is comparing to competitors
"Don't. Build for your users, not against competitors. If you make your users 10% happier, you win. The path to victory is user love, not feature comparison."

## Advanced YC Frameworks (Deep Cuts)

### Situation 5: Default Alive vs Default Dead (The Runway Reality Check)

**What it means:** A startup is either "default alive" (profitable path is clear, can reach profitability before money runs out) or "default dead" (needs external funding to survive).

**Why it matters:** Most founders optimize for growth and miss the profitability cliff. YC forces you to think about both.

**Default Alive Signal:**
- You can cut burn to $X/month and still operate
- At current burn rate, you have 18+ months of runway
- Each customer pays more than their cost to acquire
- You could be profitable in 2-3 years without more capital

**Default Dead Signal:**
- Burn rate requires scaling revenue 3x+ to survive
- Customer acquisition costs exceed first-year revenue
- Each new customer makes unit economics worse
- You NEED Series B to survive Series A

**The Framework:**
```
Monthly burn: $50k
Monthly revenue: $30k
Net burn: $20k
Runway: $500k bank / $20k = 25 months
Verdict: Default Alive (25 months > 18 month threshold)

vs.

Monthly burn: $100k
Monthly revenue: $10k
Net burn: $90k
Runway: $500k bank / $90k = 5.5 months
Verdict: Default Dead (URGENT — need to raise or cut burn NOW)
```

**YC principle:** Be default alive. Then you negotiate from strength. Investors know you can survive without them — that's powerful.

**Red flag:** "We're growing 20% MoM!" (high growth, default dead = fragile. One bad month and you're out of runway)

### Situation 6: "Startup = Growth" (Paul Graham's Definition)

**What it is:** Paul Graham defines a startup as a company designed to grow fast, not just a small business.

**The distinction matters:**
- **Small business:** Make $50k/year, keep the profit, stay small. Goal is profitability + stability.
- **Startup:** Design for 10x growth in 2-3 years. Goal is massive scalability + venture return.

**Why this changes everything:**

If you're building a startup, you optimize for GROWTH above profitability in the early years.
- Growth ≠ revenue (it's percentage growth in your key metric)
- Profitability ≠ efficiency (you'll be unprofitable if growth requires it)
- You need capital because you're investing in future scale

**Example:**
- Coffee shop: Profitable from day 1, slow growth, small business
- SaaS startup: Unprofitable for 2 years, 30% MoM growth, raises capital, targets VC exit

**The application to your thinking:**
- Are you building for growth (startup) or stability (business)?
- If growth: Optimize for unit growth metrics (MoM %, user growth rate)
- If business: Optimize for profitability (keep what you make)

**YC founder perspective:**
"Is this a business or a startup?" is the first question. Your answer determines everything about the strategy.

### Situation 7: Painkillers vs Vitamins (What People Actually Want)

**The distinction:**
- **Painkiller:** Solves an acute, immediate problem. Users NEED it now.
- **Vitamin:** Nice to have, improves something, but not urgent. Users WANT it eventually.

**Why it matters:** Painkillers grow 10x faster than vitamins.

**Examples:**

| Painkiller | Vitamin |
|---|---|
| Accounting software (painful tax season coming) | Analytics dashboard (nice insights) |
| Password manager (security anxiety) | Meditation app (self-improvement) |
| Team communication (chaos without it) | Project management (organization nice-to-have) |
| Spam filter (inbox overflowing) | Email templates (saves time) |

**How to know which you're building:**
1. Ask users: "Would you go back to doing it without this?"
2. If YES = painkiller. If NO = vitamin.
3. Painkillers have higher willingness to pay + lower churn

**The danger:** Building vitamins and pricing them like painkillers. Users won't pay for optional improvements.

**YC wisdom:** Find the painkillers. Vitamins are a second product (after painkiller succeeds).

### Situation 8: PMF Stress-Test Checklist (How to Know You Have It)

**Is your product-market fit real, or are you fooling yourself?**

**The Checklist:**

**Retention (Does it stick?)**
- [ ] >30% of users active after 1 month (B2C benchmark)
- [ ] >10% returning after 6 months (B2C)
- [ ] Churn is stable and predictable (not shocking drops)
- [ ] You can predict churn behavior by cohort

**Growth (Does it spread?)**
- [ ] Organic word-of-mouth (measured by referral source)
- [ ] Growth compounds WITHOUT paid ads (natural viral loop)
- [ ] Net Promoter Score (NPS) >50 (would users recommend?)
- [ ] Users proactively invite others

**Unit Economics (Does it make sense?)**
- [ ] LTV (lifetime value) > 3x CAC (customer acquisition cost)
- [ ] Contribution margin: >50% after COGS
- [ ] Payback period: <12 months (for B2B SaaS, <6 months)

**Product Demand (Do users actually want this?)**
- [ ] Users would be upset if you shut down (qualitative)
- [ ] Support requests are about features, not bugs (product fit, not quality)
- [ ] Price increases don't crush conversion (demand is strong)

**The Test:** If you can't check >80% of these boxes, you don't have PMF yet.

**Red flags that look like PMF but aren't:**
- ❌ High signup, low retention (awareness ≠ product fit)
- ❌ Paid growth only (ads force adoption, not fit)
- ❌ Founder enthusiasm (you're biased)
- ❌ Investor interest (investors are wrong often)
- ❌ Revenue growth without unit economics (unsustainable)

### Situation 9: Nuclear Launch Codes (Founder Commitment Signals)

**What it means:** How do you know if a founder is truly committed vs. hedging their bets?

**The signals:**
- They quit their job (skin in the game)
- They moved closer to the opportunity (dedicated)
- They've been working on the problem for months before "startups" (not opportunistic)
- They've turned down other opportunities (prioritization)
- They talk to customers constantly (obsession)

**Why YC cares:** Commitment > intelligence. Mediocre founders who commit outlast brilliant founders who hedge.

**The inverse signal (red flags):**
- ❌ Still working a day job "until we raise"
- ❌ Built it as a side project, launched to see what sticks
- ❌ Vague on why they're solving THIS problem (not personal)
- ❌ Optimizing for investor interest, not customer fit (chasing money)

**For yourself:** What are YOUR nuclear launch codes? Have you truly committed?

### Situation 10: Luck Requires Preparation

**The insight:** YC founder success looks like luck to outsiders. But Luck = Preparation × Timing × Obsession.

**What looks like luck:**
- "They were in the right market at the right time"
- "They found a technical co-founder right when they needed one"
- "A customer gave them massive feedback that changed the product"
- "An investor noticed them at the right moment"

**What actually happened (preparation):**
- They studied multiple markets and were ready when one exploded
- They BUILT the technical co-founder relationship by co-creating over months
- They were obsessively talking to customers 10x more than competitors
- They had a working product and clear metrics when they pitched

**The formula (YC thinking):**

```
Luck happens = You prepared for it + You showed up in the right place + You executed when opportunity appeared

Luck doesn't happen = You hoped things would work out + You tried once + You moved on after rejection
```

**Preparation signals:**
- You've built 3 things before (failed twice, learned)
- You know the customer deeply (not from guessing, from obsessive interviews)
- You have a working prototype (not a pitch deck)
- You can articulate your edge (why you specifically will win, not just "the market is big")

**How to increase your luck:**
1. **Study the market:** Know trends 6-12 months ahead. When something explodes, you're already thinking about it.
2. **Build your network:** Not for fundraising, but for early feedback. Your strongest signal comes from trusted people, not from surveys.
3. **Execute projects:** Each project teaches you something. Founders with 10 failed projects have 10x the edge of first-time founders.
4. **Stay obsessed:** When you find a customer problem you care about, go deep. Most people stay shallow.
5. **Be ready to move fast:** When opportunity appears (market timing, key hire available, customer wants to buy), can you move in 48 hours?

**The luck paradox:** YC founder success isn't about getting lucky. It's about being so prepared that when luck appears, you're the only one ready to catch it.

**Example timeline:**
```
Year 1: You spend 9 months learning a market (customers, problems, solutions)
Year 2: You build a solution, talk to 50 customers, iterate
Year 3: Market shifts, your prepared insight becomes urgent, adoption explodes
  → Looks like luck (right time, right place)
  → Actually: 2 years of preparation making you ready

vs.

Year 1: You have a vague idea, build for 3 months, launch
Year 2: Market shifts, you're not ready to pivot, get left behind
  → Looks like bad luck
  → Actually: Not prepared enough
```
