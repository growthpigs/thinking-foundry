## Overview

Design Thinking is not about aesthetics. It's a **human-centered problem-solving framework** that forces empathy before ideation, prototyping before commitment, and iteration over perfection.

IDEO's insight: **Most product failures happen because founders solve for the wrong problem or solve for the average user instead of deeply understanding specific humans.**

**Core philosophy:**
> "Design thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation that integrates what is desirable from a human point of view with what is technologically feasible and economically viable." — Tim Brown, IDEO

**In a Thinking Foundry session,** Design Thinking prevents you from building the wrong product by forcing you to START with the user, not your assumptions about the user.

## Core Frameworks & Principles

### 1. The Five Phases of Design Thinking

**The Sequential Process:**

#### Phase A: Empathize (Understand the User)
**Goal:** Deeply understand the human you're solving for—not demographics, but motivations, frustrations, values, context.

**Methods:**
- **User interviews** (1-on-1, open-ended, no leading questions)
- **Contextual inquiry** (observe the user in their environment doing the task)
- **Diary studies** (users document their own experience over time)
- **Journey mapping** (timeline of user's experience: moments of joy, frustration, surprise)

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Survey 1,000 people (breadth, not depth)
- ❌ Ask "would you use this?" (they'll say yes; test with prototypes)
- ❌ Assume you know the user (founders are rarely the user)

**Red flag:** If you haven't interviewed 5+ actual users in their context, you don't have empathy yet.

**Example:**
- ❌ Assumption: "Small businesses need better time-tracking software"
- ✓ Empathy: After 5 interviews, discover: "Small business owners hate time-tracking because it makes them feel watched. They actually want trust and autonomy."
- This insight changes EVERYTHING about the product.

#### Phase B: Define (Problem Statement)
**Goal:** Reframe your understanding into a **clear, empathy-driven problem statement**, not a solution.

**The statement format:**
```
[User type] needs [need] because [insight from empathy].
```

**Example:**
- ❌ "Build a time-tracking app"
- ✓ "Freelancers need transparency about how they spend time WITHOUT feeling monitored, because they value autonomy and trust with clients"

**Key principle:** The problem statement should inspire multiple solution approaches, not lead to one obvious solution.

**Testing the statement:**
- Does it reflect what you learned in Empathize? (tie back to user interviews)
- Does it suggest the scope (too broad? too narrow?)
- Could this problem be solved 5 different ways? (if yes, it's good; if it leads to one solution, it's too narrow)

#### Phase C: Ideate (Generate Possibilities)
**Goal:** Generate MANY ideas without judgment, then cluster and evaluate.

**The rules of ideation:**
1. **Defer judgment** — say yes to wild ideas first, evaluate later
2. **Encourage wild ideas** — constraints come later; creativity first
3. **Build on others' ideas** — "Yes, and..." not "No, but..."
4. **Stay focused on topic** — keep coming back to the problem statement
5. **One conversation at a time** — listen deeply, don't multitask
6. **Be visual** — sketch, don't just talk

**Ideation techniques:**
- **Brainstorm:** Rapid-fire ideas (30 min, no judgment)
- **Bodystorm:** Act out scenarios (people role-play the user experience)
- **Six thinking hats:** Different perspectives (optimist, pessimist, creative, logical, etc.)
- **Worst possible idea:** Start with "how to make this WORSE?" then invert
- **Attribute listing:** List attributes of the problem, swap them with random things, see what sticks

**The output:** 50+ ideas (most will be bad; that's the point). Then cluster into themes and pick 3-5 to prototype.

**Red flag:** If you only have 5-10 ideas, you didn't ideate enough. Go back.

#### Phase D: Prototype (Build to Test)
**Goal:** Make the idea REAL (even crude) so you can test it with users. Fidelity depends on what you need to learn.

**Prototype types by fidelity:**

| Fidelity | What it is | When to use | Time |
|----------|-----------|------------|------|
| **Low** | Sketch, role-play, storyboard | Early validation of concept | 1-4 hours |
| **Medium** | Paper prototype, clickable wireframe, Figma mockup | Testing user flow and key moments | 1-3 days |
| **High** | Functional MVP, video simulation, actual product | Before major investment, testing at scale | 1-4 weeks |

**Principle:** Build just enough to test what you need to learn. **No more.** The goal is learning, not a finished product.

**The prototype mindset:**
- ✓ "What's the minimum we need to test?"
- ✓ "Can we test this with a Figma prototype instead of code?"
- ✓ "Can we role-play this instead of building it?"
- ❌ "Let's build the full product to show investors"

**Example flows:**
- Testing if freelancers want "transparency" feature: low-fidelity sketch + 5-min user test
- Testing user flow through onboarding: Figma clickthrough + 15-min user test
- Testing if the business model works at scale: MVP + 50 paying customers

#### Phase E: Test (Learn & Iterate)
**Goal:** Get feedback from users on your prototype, extract insights, iterate.

**Testing rules:**
1. **Observe more than listen** — watch what the user does, not just what they say
2. **Ask "why?"** — go deeper than surface answers
3. **Look for moments of delight and frustration** — where does the user smile? Where do they squint?
4. **Capture quotes** — word-for-word what the user says about moments
5. **Don't defend your idea** — listen to criticism, don't explain

**The output:** Not "Is this good?" but "What did we learn? What should we change?"

**Iteration patterns:**
- Learning type A: "Users love X, but they hate Y" → iterate Y
- Learning type B: "Users don't understand the core value prop" → redesign messaging
- Learning type C: "This solves the problem, but it's too expensive" → simplify or find cheaper delivery
- Learning type D: "This solves a different problem than we thought" → pivot the problem statement

**Returning to Design Thinking:**
After testing, you loop back:
- Empathize: Did we understand the user correctly?
- Define: Should we adjust the problem statement?
- Ideate: Do we have a new direction?
- Prototype: What should we test next?

**Red flag:** If you're not changing your prototype based on user feedback, you're not testing; you're confirming.

### 2. The Design Thinking Mindset (Not Just Process)

**Core mindset shifts:**

| From | To |
|------|-----|
| "Build the product" | "Understand the problem" |
| "Feature-focused" | "Need-focused" |
| "One solution" | "Multiple possible solutions" |
| "Assumption-driven" | "Evidence-driven" |
| "Polished prototype" | "Scrappy learning prototype" |
| "User feedback confirms" | "User feedback challenges" |

**The beginner's mistake:** Founders treat Design Thinking as a 5-step process to get to a product. Actually, it's a **mindset** where you're always returning to empathy, questioning your assumptions, and building to learn.

### 3. Empathy Mapping (Goes Deeper Than Interviews)

**The Tool:** A structured way to organize what you learned about a user.

**The six sections:**

```
╔════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║     WHO IS THE USER? (Demographics)        ║
╠════════════════════════════════════════════╣
║ SEES:               │ HEARS:               ║
║ [Market, ads,      │ [What people say,    ║
║ what's around      │ friend feedback,     ║
║ them]              │ media messages]      ║
├────────────────────┼────────────────────┤
║ THINKS & FEELS:     │ SAYS & DOES:         ║
║ [Worries, hopes,   │ [Behavior, public   ║
║ aspirations]       │ statements]         ║
├────────────────────┼────────────────────┤
║ PAINS:              │ GAINS:               ║
║ [Fears, frustrations│[Desired outcomes,   ║
║ obstacles]          │what success looks   ║
║                     │like]                ║
╚════════════════════════════════════════════╝
```

**How to fill it:**
1. Pick a specific user (not "all small business owners" but "Sarah, freelance designer in Berlin")
2. Interview Sarah for 45 minutes
3. Fill each section directly from what you learned
4. Use direct quotes, not paraphrases
5. Identify the **gap between what she SAYS and what she THINKS** (the insight)

**The insight:** Often in the gap between "says" and "thinks." She says "I need better tools" but thinks "I don't have time to learn new tools."

**Example (Time Tracking):**
```
SEES: Competitors launching AI tools, articles about "automation", her clients asking "how many hours?"
HEARS: "You should track time", "Are you using the latest software?", friend's frustration with Toggl
THINKS: "I hate being measured like a robot. Am I spending time wrong?"
SAYS: "I'll track my time for a month"
PAINS: Feeling micromanaged, losing trust with clients, complexity of tools
GAINS: Building trust through transparency, understanding where time actually goes

INSIGHT: She wants trust with clients without feeling monitored. The solution isn't "more tools" but "transparency with autonomy."
```

### 4. The User Journey Map (Beyond Empathy Mapping)

**What it is:** Timeline of user's experience from START to END, including moments of emotion and pain.

**Why it matters:** Shows you where to focus innovation. Usually, the biggest opportunities are at moments of peak frustration.

**Example (Freelancer using time-tracking for first time):**

```
1. AWARENESS
   [Sees article about time-tracking]
   Emotion: Neutral. Thought: "Maybe I should try this"

2. SETUP
   [Installs app, creates account, reads tutorial]
   Emotion: Frustrated. Pain: Too many options, unclear what to do
   Opportunity: Simplified onboarding

3. FIRST USE
   [Starts tracking a project]
   Emotion: Confused. Pain: Forgetting to stop timer, unsure about how to categorize
   Opportunity: Auto-detection, smart defaults

4. REVIEW
   [Looks at reports at end of week]
   Emotion: Anxious. Pain: "I tracked 20 hours on the client project but invoiced for 15. Do I tell them?"
   Opportunity: CORE INSIGHT — The product forced a conversation they weren't ready for

5. DECISION
   [Decides to stop using it]
   Emotion: Guilty. Pain: Felt like a failure to use the tool
   Opportunity: Repositioning as "optional insight", not "required discipline"
```

**The key finding:** The original problem ("I need time tracking") wasn't the real problem. The real problem was "I'm anxious about having uncomfortable conversations with clients about my actual time investment."

### 5. Rapid Experimentation (The IDEO Speed)

**Principle:** Get to prototypes FAST (hours, not weeks).

**The speed loop:**

```
Monday AM: Define problem statement (1 hour)
Monday afternoon: Brainstorm (2 hours)
Tuesday AM: Build 3 prototypes (3-4 hours total)
Tuesday PM: Test with 3 users (2 hours)
Wednesday AM: Iterate (2-3 hours)
Wednesday PM: Test again (1-2 hours)

Total: 3 days to validated insights
```

**How to move fast:**
- Use existing tools (Figma, not code; paper, not Figma; roleplay, not paper)
- Recruit users EARLY (before you have a prototype, tell them "I want to show you something and get feedback")
- Make decisions quickly (good enough now beats perfect later)
- Embrace constraints (limited budget = creative solutions)

## When NOT to Use Design Thinking (Critical)

Design Thinking excels at **novel problems and human needs**. It fails at:
- ❌ Pure infrastructure problems (database optimization)
- ❌ Compliance/regulatory work (you don't have flexibility)
- ❌ Problems where users are wrong about what they need (AI/emerging tech)
- ❌ When time is critical (Design Thinking takes 4-12 weeks; emergency fixes don't)

**When to combine:** Design Thinking + Lean (test + measure + pivot fast)

## When to Apply (Phase by Phase)

| Phase | IDEO Application | Example |
|-------|------------------|---------|
| **0 - User Stories** | Empathize with your user; map their journey | "What is Sarah's real obstacle? Not what she SAYS, what does she actually FEEL?" |
| **1 - MINE** | Empathy mapping; identify the real problem | "We thought they wanted X, but they actually fear Y" |
| **2 - SCOUT** | Ideate multiple possible solutions to the real problem | "Given what we learned, here are 5 radically different approaches" |
| **3 - ASSAY** | Design the solution that best serves the user | "Which solution aligns with user values and willingness to pay?" |
| **4 - CRUCIBLE** | Prototype and test the key value proposition | "Do users actually value this, or are we assuming?" |
| **5 - AUDITOR** | Test messaging and go-to-market approach | "Does the marketing language match what users actually want?" |
| **6 - PLAN** | Plan based on what you've learned about the user | "What's the user journey we're optimizing for?" |
| **7 - VERIFY** | Did the product match the user we empathized with? | "Did we build for the real user, or the imaginary one?" |

## Common Founder Mistakes (And IDEO Solutions)

### Mistake 1: Building for the Average User
- **Problem:** You survey 100 people, find 60% want feature X, build it
- **IDEO solution:** Interview 3-5 deeply, get into their context. You'll find that the "average" doesn't exist; different segments have radically different needs. Serve one segment deeply.

### Mistake 2: Features Over Needs
- **Problem:** You build a feature-rich product that does everything
- **IDEO solution:** Start with ONE unmet need. Solve that perfectly. Add features only when users ask (and even then, ask WHY).

### Mistake 3: Prototyping Too Late
- **Problem:** You spend 3 months building, then show users and learn you misunderstood
- **IDEO solution:** Prototype after 1 week (paper or Figma). Test with 3 users. Learn. Adjust. THEN build.

### Mistake 4: Feedback Confirmation, Not Challenge
- **Problem:** "Users loved our demo!" (They were being polite)
- **IDEO solution:** Watch what they do, not what they say. Look for moments of confusion. Ask "why?" to everything. Expect them to be wrong about what they want.

## Example AI Prompts

### If founder is building without user research
"Before building, we need empathy. Can you tell me about 3 actual conversations with customers? Not surveys — conversations where you watched them, asked why, and discovered something surprising?"

### If founder is adding features
"Every feature should solve a specific user need. Can you trace this feature back to what user problem it solves? If you can't point to a specific user quote, it's a solution looking for a problem."

### If founder is showing a prototype
"Great. Now watch 3 actual users try to use it without explanation. Where do they get stuck? Where do they smile? The confusion moments are where we innovate next."

### If founder is pivoting
"Before pivoting, have you re-empathized? Does your new direction still serve the user you discovered? Or are you chasing a different problem?"

### 6. The "How Might We" Technique (Reframing Problems Into Opportunities)

**The insight:** The way you phrase a problem determines what solutions you'll find. "How might we?" rephrases constraints as creative possibilities.

**The problem with problem statements:**
- ❌ "We need to reduce customer churn" → implies cutting costs
- ❌ "Our onboarding is confusing" → implies UI design fix only
- ✓ "How might we make customers feel so welcome they never want to leave?" → implies culture, experience, relationship

**HMW Format:**
```
How might we [reframe the constraint as an opportunity]?
```

**Example transformations:**

| Original Problem | HMW Reframe | Mindset Shift |
|---|---|---|
| "Users forget to use the app" | "How might we make the app so useful that they crave it?" | From reminder-pushing to value creation |
| "Customer support is expensive" | "How might we turn support into a delightful brand moment?" | From cost-cutting to competitive advantage |
| "Founders don't know how to pitch" | "How might we make pitching feel natural and authentic?" | From rigid rules to expression |
| "Onboarding takes too long" | "How might we make the first 5 minutes so compelling they can't stop?" | From process efficiency to experience design |

**Why it works:** HMW gives your brain permission to be creative while staying focused on the real constraint.

**How to generate HMW statements (Brainstorm format):**
1. Start with the problem: "Users churn after week 1"
2. Ask "Why?" 5 times to get to root: "Because they don't see value yet"
3. Reframe as opportunity: "How might we help them see value in the first 3 days?"
4. Generate 10 HMW variations by adjusting the focus:
   - "How might we onboard them gradually instead of dumping everything on day 1?"
   - "How might we celebrate their first win, no matter how small?"
   - "How might we show them success stories of people like them?"
   - "How might we let them experience the outcome before committing?"
5. Pick the HMW that feels most aligned with your user insight

**HMW in a Thinking Foundry session:**
When a founder is stuck: "You've identified the constraint. Now, how MIGHT we turn this into an opportunity? What's the inverse of this problem?"

### 7. Analogous Inspiration (Solving Problems from Other Domains)

**The principle:** The best solutions to your problem might already exist in a completely different industry. IDEO solves problems by finding analogies.

**Why analogies work:** Different industries have solved similar human needs in different ways. A design system problem might be solved by looking at how hospital workflows organize information.

**How to find analogies:**

**Step 1: Identify the core human need (not the surface problem)**
- Surface problem: "Freelancers forget to track time"
- Core need: "Humans need small, frequent reminders to capture important moments"

**Step 2: Find industries solving the same need**
- Insurance: Captures small claims efficiently → Autopsy form structure
- Fitness apps: Capture tiny workouts → Streak gamification
- Museums: Capture visitor movement → Breadcrumb trails
- Restaurants: Capture order preferences → Muscle memory shortcuts

**Step 3: Adapt the solution to your context**
- Fitness app's streak mechanic → "Track 7 consecutive days of time entry" gamification
- Insurance form structure → "3-field capture" vs "20-field form" (why does insurance form work despite being boring?)
- Breadcrumb trails → Visual timeline showing "You've tracked X% of this week"

**Real IDEO example:** Designing a better hospital experience for patients
- Analogous domain 1: Hotels (hospitality + wayfinding) → Inspired calming lobby design
- Analogous domain 2: Airports (clarity under stress) → Inspired visual wayfinding (patients are stressed, need obvious directions)
- Analogous domain 3: Restaurants (multi-sensory experience) → Inspired adding plants/art (sensory experience improves emotional state)
- Result: Hospital felt less clinical, patients reported lower anxiety

**Analogous inspiration for Thinking Foundry:**
"This founder's problem is helping users trust an AI system with their thinking. Let's look at:
- How therapists build trust (consistency, listening, confidentiality)
- How pilots trust automated systems (checklists, override capability, transparency)
- How teachers establish classroom trust (explaining their reasoning, admitting mistakes)"

**The analogous search process (Fast version):**
```
1. What's the user's core need?
   "Feel heard and understood"

2. What other domains address this need?
   Therapists, coaches, mentors, best friends, confessionals, support groups

3. Pick one: How do therapists build trust?
   Active listening, asking follow-up questions, remembering details, avoiding judgment

4. Adapt to your context:
   "Our AI should ask follow-up questions it discovered in previous sessions"
   "Our AI should remember details about the founder's situation"
   "Our AI should avoid being preachy or judgmental"
```

**Anti-pattern (What doesn't work):**
- ❌ Finding analogies that are too surface-level ("Uber uses location, so should we")
- ❌ Forcing analogies that don't exist ("Nothing analogous to our problem")
- ✓ Finding analogies at the human-need level, then adapting the system design
