## Who is Nate B. Jones

Nate B. Jones is a strategic thinking practitioner focused on structured decision-making, mental models, and cognitive frameworks for entrepreneurs. His approach synthesizes classical strategic thinking with practical application — the bridge between academic frameworks and real-world decision velocity.

**Core contribution:** Not *what* to think about, but *how* to think about it. The meta-framework.

In a Thinking Foundry session, Nate's frameworks help organize your cognitive process, avoid reasoning traps, and build defensible arguments for decisions made under uncertainty.

## Core Frameworks & Principles

### 1. The Thinking Stack (Your Personal Cognitive Architecture)

**What it is:** A curated set of mental models organized in three layers that you apply consistently to every decision.

**Layer 1: Perception (How you see the problem)**

**First Principles Thinking:**
- Strip away layers of abstraction and assumptions
- Ask: "What is actually, provably true here?"
- Rebuild your understanding from fundamentals

Example: "We need better time-tracking software"
- ❌ Surface layer: "Software helps people track time"
- ✓ First principles: "People struggle to remember what they did yesterday. Tracking forces them to pay attention. What if they just paid more attention in real-time?"
- Insight: The problem isn't software, it's awareness

**Inversion:**
- Instead of "How do I succeed?" ask "How would I guarantee failure?"
- List every way the project could fail
- Systematically prevent each failure

Example: "How would I guarantee this time-tracking business fails?"
- No users sign up (pricing too high)
- Users sign up but don't return (product doesn't work)
- Users return but don't pay (no perceived value)
- Users pay but we burn through money (unit economics broken)
Now prevent each one.

**Second-Order Thinking:**
- First order: "I launch this product, users like it"
- Second order: "Users like it, then word spreads, then competitors notice"
- Third order: "Competitors copy, market becomes crowded, I'm no longer differentiated"
- Fourth order: "How do I stay ahead in a crowded market?"

This thinking changes the strategy from "just launch" to "how do I build defensible moats?"

**Layer 2: Analysis (How you evaluate options)**

**Opportunity Cost Awareness:**
- Every "yes" is a "no" to something else
- Not explicit: If you spend 6 months building feature A, that's 6 months you're NOT building feature B or improving customer service

Quantify it: "If I do option A ($X cost), what am I giving up?" Forced comparison.

**Reversibility Test (The Key Decision Filter):**

| Decision Type | Reversibility | Decision Time | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Type 1 (One-Way Door)** | Hard to undo | Take days, consult others | Hiring, raising capital, product pivot, major marketing |
| **Type 2 (Two-Way Door)** | Easy to undo | Decide in hours | Marketing test, small feature, new vendor |

**The insight:** Most founders treat Type 2 decisions like Type 1. This creates decision fatigue.

Decision speed by type:
- ✓ Type 1: Slow, deliberate, lots of input (but not paralyzed)
- ✓ Type 2: Fast (2 hours), but monitor and be ready to reverse

**Asymmetric Upside:**
Look for bets where:
- Downside is limited (you can afford to lose it)
- Upside is 10x the downside

Example:
- Option A: Invest $10k in marketing. Best case 50% return ($5k), worst case -$10k. EV = -$2.5k (bad)
- Option B: Invest $10k in product. Best case 300% return ($30k), worst case -$5k. EV = $17.5k (good)

Choose B despite higher risk.

**Layer 3: Action (How you decide and move)**

**Decision Velocity:**
- Speed of decision matters more than precision for most decisions
- Analysis paralysis = you're making a decision to NOT decide (wrong decision, wrong speed)
- Rule: Decide when you have 70% of the information you'd ideally like

**Regret Minimization:**
- Imagine yourself at 80, looking back
- Which choice would you regret NOT taking?
- Usually points to the harder, more interesting path (not the safe path)

**Commitment Escalation Awareness:**
- Are you continuing because it's right? Or because you've already invested?
- Classic: "I've spent 6 months on this feature, I have to ship it" (sunk cost)
- Reality: Those 6 months are gone. Separate that from the decision of whether to ship.

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Let's think through this in layers. First, perception: how are we framing this problem? Is there a different lens? Second, analysis: what's the opportunity cost of each option? Is this Type 1 or Type 2? Third, action: which decision would you regret NOT taking at 80?"

### 2. The Inversion Principle (Failure-Resistant Design)

**What it is:** Instead of asking "How do I succeed?" ask "How would I guarantee failure?" Then design to avoid every failure mode.

Charlie Munger: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I never go there."

**Why it's powerful:**
- Positive thinking: "What could succeed?" (too many possibilities, impossible to cover all)
- Inversion: "What could fail?" (smaller set, more concrete, actionable)

**The process:**

**Step 1: Identify all failure modes**
```
Question: "How would I guarantee this product fails?"

Failure modes for a SaaS startup:
- No customers sign up (marketing doesn't work)
- Customers sign up but don't use (UX is bad)
- Customers use but don't renew (value doesn't stick)
- Customers renew but churn accelerates (product stales)
- Customers stay but cost too much to acquire (unit economics broken)
- Customers stay and pay but we run out of money (burn rate too high)
```

**Step 2: Categorize by control**
```
Within our control:
- Build a bad product (we control this)
- Waste money (we control this)
- Demoralize the team (we control this)

Outside our control:
- Market disappears (market risk)
- Recession (macro risk)
- Competitor with unlimited capital (competitive risk)
```

**Step 3: Prevention mechanisms**
```
Failure mode: "Customers don't renew"
→ Causes:
   a) Product value isn't clear
   b) Product breaks after update
   c) Competitor has better option
→ Prevention:
   a) Customer feedback every month (not quarterly)
   b) Regression testing for every release
   c) Run competitive analysis quarterly, have 1 differentiator we own
```

**Step 4: Accept the uncontrollable ones (Stoic integration)**
```
Recession: "If there's a downturn, we'll cut burn to X/month, focus on profitability, extend runway"
Unlimited competitor: "We can't outspend them, so we own a specific niche better than anyone"
```

**Real example: Stripe's inversion**
```
How would Stripe fail?
- Developers don't adopt (build great API docs, respond in minutes to support)
- Developers adopt but churn (pricing is fair, support never lets them down)
- Developers stay but we can't scale (hire ops-focused, build tooling, not just product)
```

**The output:** A plan that's failure-resistant by design, not just hope.

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Let's invert. How would you guarantee this fails spectacularly? List every way. Now for each one, what prevents it? Your plan should prevent every preventable failure."

### 3. Decision Quality Framework (The Type 1 / Type 2 Matrix)

**Not all decisions deserve the same cognitive investment.**

**Decision Matrix: Impact × Reversibility**

| | **Easy to Undo (Reversible)** | **Hard to Undo (Irreversible)** |
|---|---|---|
| **High Impact** | Try it, monitor closely (2-4 week trial period) | Full analysis, get input, deliberate (days) |
| **Low Impact** | Delegate or decide in <1 hour | Decide in hours, don't agonize |

**Real examples:**

**Type 1 (One-Way Door):**
- Hiring a VP of Sales (hard to undo, high impact) → spend 2 weeks, interview 20 people, get board input
- Pricing decision change (impacts all customers) → analyze data, A/B test, decide once
- Product pivot (might alienate existing customers) → full impact analysis, pivot is deliberate

**Type 2 (Two-Way Door):**
- Try new marketing channel ($5k spend) → A/B test, measure for 2 weeks, kill if not working
- Reorganize team structure → try for 30 days, revert if it doesn't work
- New vendor for service → test during trial period, switch back if bad

**The insight:** 80% of decisions are Type 2. Founders treat them like Type 1 → decision paralysis.

**The decision speed by type:**
- Type 1: Slow (but not paralyzed) — 3-7 days, get input, deliberate
- Type 2: FAST — 2-4 hours, decide, commit, monitor, be ready to reverse

**Decision cost of being wrong:**
- Type 1 wrong: Could cost the company
- Type 2 wrong: Cost a week of lost time, learn something

**Rule:** If the cost of being wrong is <$50k and reversible in <2 weeks, it's Type 2. Decide in a meeting.

### 4. The Compounding Framework (Assets vs. Treadmills)

**Core insight:** Most people think in linear terms ("I do X effort, I get Y result"). In reality, some choices create exponential returns (compounding) while others are eternal treadmills.

**Positive Compounding (Creates Assets):**

**Knowledge Compounding:**
- Year 1: Learn marketing (hard, slow)
- Year 2: Learn sales (easier, because you understand customer psychology from marketing)
- Year 3: Learn pricing (even easier, now you understand value and customer acquisition costs)
- Year 5: You're 100x more effective at any growth problem

**Relationship Compounding:**
- Year 1: Build 50 relationships (lots of networking)
- Year 2: Your 50 relationships introduce you to their network (250 new relationships via introductions)
- Year 3: Your 300 relationships are now resources for advice, customers, hires, capital

**Content Compounding:**
- Write 50 articles on a topic
- Google surfaces all 50 (one article doesn't; 50 does)
- Each article drives traffic that compounds (people share, link, quote)
- You're still getting traffic from articles written 2 years ago

**Systems Compounding:**
- Build a hiring process
- Next hire is faster (you've optimized the process)
- 5 hires later, you've automated 80% of screening
- System is now an asset

**Negative Compounding (Things to Avoid):**

**Technical Debt:**
- Today: Skip testing to ship fast (saves 1 week)
- Day 30: Bug in production (takes 3 days to fix, would've been caught by tests)
- Day 60: Can't add new features without breaking old ones (takes 2x longer per feature)
- Day 90: Can't onboard new engineers (codebase is too fragile)

**Broken Trust:**
- First broken promise: People give you 2nd chance
- Second broken promise: They're skeptical
- Third: You're not taken seriously anymore
- Fourth: You're out

**Bad Habits:**
- Skip exercise for 1 week (no visible impact)
- Skip for 1 month (you feel worse)
- Skip for 3 months (health declines, energy drops, productivity suffers)
- Hard to recover

**The decision framework:**
When choosing between options, ask: "Which creates a compounding asset? Which is a treadmill?"

Example:
- Option A: Hire a generalist to do lots of tasks (immediate help, but no leverage)
- Option B: Invest in automation/systems (slower now, but compounds over time)

For a 5+ year business, Option B compounds better.

**Time horizon matters:**
- Short term (<6 months): Treadmill options OK (hire, push hard)
- Medium term (1-3 years): Mix (some treadmill, some compounding)
- Long term (5+ years): Optimize for compounding (systems, content, relationships, knowledge)

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Which option creates an asset that's more valuable next year? Which is a treadmill you have to be on forever? Prefer the asset."

### 5. The Leverage Framework (Scaling Without Scaling Yourself)

**What it is:** Four types of leverage that multiply your impact. Start with permissionless (code + media), then unlock capital + labor.

**Type 1: Labor Leverage**
- Definition: People working for you
- Example: CEO directing 100 people × 100 = 100x leverage
- Pros: Can scale big
- Cons: Hard to manage, depends on good hiring, takes trust + capital
- When to use: Post-PMF when you have money and need to scale fast

**Type 2: Capital Leverage**
- Definition: Money working for you
- Example: $1M in the bank, earning 5% = $50k/year passive
- Pros: Compounds indefinitely
- Cons: Requires capital first (catch-22), subject to market forces
- When to use: After you've made money or raised capital, this becomes critical

**Type 3: Code Leverage (High Priority for Startups)**
- Definition: Software working for you 24/7, zero marginal cost
- Example: Software you build works for 1 or 1M users with same cost
- Pros: Scales infinitely, permissionless (no one has to hire you), compounds over time
- Cons: Takes time to build, requires technical skill
- When to use: NOW — as early as possible. This is the modern startup moat

**Type 4: Media Leverage (High Priority for Startups)**
- Definition: Content working for you, builds while you sleep
- Example: Blog post written today attracts customers in Year 2 and Year 5
- Pros: Permissionless, compounds, builds trust/authority
- Cons: Slow to start, requires consistency
- When to use: Parallel with code. Start building your authority day 1

**Leverage Priority by Stage:**

**Earliest (Pre-PMF):** Code + Media
- Code: Build the product (permission needed from customers, not society)
- Media: Start documenting your thinking/learnings (free, builds audience)

**Growth (Post-PMF):** Code + Media + Labor
- Labor: Hire people to scale what works
- Code + Media: Still your moats

**Mature:** Code + Media + Labor + Capital
- Capital: Invest in growth, compounding, new ventures
- Others: Still active

**The framework:**
When evaluating a path, ask:
- Does this give me code leverage (product that scales infinitely)?
- Does this give me media leverage (content that compounds)?
- Or am I trading my time forever (treadmill)?

**Real example:**
- Path A: Freelance consulting ($150k/year, but you do the work)
  - Leverage type: Labor (selling your time)
  - Compounds? No

- Path B: Build software + write about building software ($150k/year today)
  - Leverage type: Code + Media
  - Compounds? Yes, exponentially

Path B is better long-term despite similar short-term income.

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "What leverage does this create? Are we building something that scales without us? Or are we trading time forever?"

## Advanced Mental Models (Supporting Your Thinking Stack)

### 1. The Map Is Not the Territory (Model Humility)

**Definition:** Your plan is a simplified representation. Reality is far more complex.

**The principle:**
- Maps are useful (they guide thinking)
- Maps are incomplete (they simplify reality)
- Reality always diverges from the map
- Hold plans loosely

**In practice:**
- ✓ Make detailed plans (they organize thinking and align the team)
- ✓ Commit to the plan short-term (alignment + execution speed)
- ✗ Mistake: Treat the plan as gospel (it's outdated the moment it's written)

**Example:**
- Plan: "We'll grow 20% MoM for 12 months" (nice, linear)
- Reality: "Month 1: 20%, Month 2: 5%, Month 3: 35%, Month 4: -10%, Month 5: 50%"
- Response: "The plan was wrong. Update it. Keep moving."

### 2. Circle of Competence (Know Your Limits)

**Definition:** Know what you know, know what you don't know, and know the difference.

**The three zones:**

| Zone | What to do | What NOT to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Core (You're expert)** | Execute with confidence. Make fast decisions. | Don't second-guess yourself |
| **Adjacent (You can learn)** | Study, learn, apprentice. Expand here. | Don't pretend expertise yet |
| **Far away (You don't understand)** | Hire/partner. Admit ignorance. Get help. | Don't pretend you understand. Ever. |

**Founder trap:** "I understand everything" (Dunning-Kruger + startup ego = disaster)

**The rule:**
- Stay in core: Move fast
- Expand adjacent: Deliberately learn (take 5-10% focus)
- Partner for far: Don't fake it

**Example:**
```
You: Expert in customer acquisition
Adjacent: Sales (similar skill set, different execution)
Far away: Hardware manufacturing (partner, don't do it yourself)

Mistake: "I'll learn hardware manufacturing and build it ourselves"
→ Cost: 10x the budget, 2x the timeline, team demoralization
Better: "We'll partner with a manufacturer. Our edge is in customer acquisition."
```

### 3. Probabilistic Thinking (Replace Certainty with EV)

**Stop:** "This will work" vs. "This won't work" (binary thinking)
**Start:** "What's the probability and expected value?"

**Framework:**

```
Option A: New market entry
Probability of success: 40%
If success: $10M revenue
Probability of failure: 60%
If failure: -$2M sunk
Expected value: (0.4 × $10M) + (0.6 × -$2M) = $4M - $1.2M = $2.8M

Option B: Optimize existing market
Probability of success: 75%
If success: $5M revenue
Probability of failure: 25%
If failure: -$500k
Expected value: (0.75 × $5M) + (0.25 × -$500k) = $3.75M - $125k = $3.625M

Option A has higher EV ($2.8M vs $3.625M means Option B is actually better)
```

**This changes decisions:** You don't minimize risk; you maximize expected value based on probabilities.

**Practice:**
- Always ask: "What's the probability this works?"
- Don't estimate 0% or 100% (wrong on both ends)
- Be realistic about odds
- Calculate EV, not just "which is riskier"

### 4. Hanlon's Razor (Assume Incompetence Over Malice)

> "Never attribute to malice that which can be adequately explained by incompetence."

**Application:** Competitor launches similar product.
- ❌ Thinking: "They're copying us! This is war!"
- ✓ Thinking: "They had a similar idea. We're validating the market. Most likely they're just working on their problem."

**In practice:**
- 99% of the time: People are fumbling through their own problems
- 1% of the time: Someone is actually scheming
- Default: Assume 99, verify before acting on 1%

### 5. The Lindy Effect (Prefer Time-Tested Over New)

**Definition:** Things that have survived a long time will likely continue to survive.

**Principle:**
- 500-year-old business model: Likely survives next 500 years
- 2-year-old business model: Likely survives next 2 years
- Time is a screening mechanism for robustness

**Application:**
- New tech (AI, blockchain): Verify via your own testing. Don't trust age alone.
- Ancient wisdom (compounding, focus, shipping): Trust. Has survived thousands of years for a reason.
- Modern trends (viral growth hack, trending): Skeptical. What was hot last year?

**In startup context:**
- Prefer proven business models (SaaS, marketplace) over new models (crypto, Web3 startups — jury still out)
- Don't ignore new tech, but require more evidence
- For culture/team: Old patterns (accountability, clarity, consistency) outperform new philosophies

### 6. The Eisenhower Matrix (Apply Ruthlessly)

| | **Urgent** | **Not Urgent** |
|---|---|---|
| **Important** | DO FIRST (crises, deadlines) | **SCHEDULE** (growth lives here) |
| **Not Important** | DELEGATE (or eliminate) | ELIMINATE (time wasters) |

**The insight:** Most people spend all day in Urgent (both important and unimportant). Growth and compounding live in Important + Not Urgent.

**Example time allocation:**
- 30% Urgent Important (customer crisis, deal about to close)
- 10% Urgent Not Important (meetings that could be emails)
- 50% Not Urgent Important ← **THIS IS WHERE YOU SHOULD BE**
  - Learning new skill
  - Building a system that scales
  - Writing that compounds
  - Relationship building
- 10% Not Urgent Not Important (eliminate these)

## Example AI Prompts

### Phase 0 (User Stories)
- "Before we dive in, let's check our framing. You described this as [X problem]. What if we reframed it as [Y opportunity]? Does that change what solutions look like?"
- "Is this a Type 1 decision (one-way door, irreversible) or Type 2 (two-way door, reversible)? That determines how much analysis we need."

### Phase 1 (MINE)
- "Let's invert. How would you guarantee this fails? List every way this could go catastrophically wrong. Now let's make sure our plan avoids each one."
- "Let's go to first principles. Strip away everything you've been told about this space. What is fundamentally, provably true? Build up from there."

### Phase 2 (SCOUT)
- "Which of these options gives you code or media leverage -- something that works for you at zero marginal cost? Those compound."
- "Thinking in terms of compounding: in 3 years, which of these options creates an asset that's more valuable than today? Which one is a treadmill?"

### Phase 4 (CRUCIBLE)
- "Second-order consequences. You choose this path. And then what? What happens next? And after that? Follow it three steps out."
- "What's the opportunity cost of this choice? What are you specifically saying 'no' to by saying 'yes' to this?"

### Phase 7 (VERIFY)
- "Imagine yourself at 80, looking back. Which decision would you regret not taking? That's probably the right one."
- "Final check: are we operating within our circle of competence here, or are we guessing? Where do we need a partner or advisor?"

## When NOT to Apply Nate Thinking (Critical)

Nate's frameworks excel at **strategic thinking and decisions under uncertainty.**

They're less useful for:
- ❌ Tactical execution (Hormozi/Lean is better)
- ❌ User empathy (IDEO is better)
