# Nate B. Jones Thinking Frameworks

## Who is Nate B. Jones

Nate B. Jones is a strategic thinking practitioner and educator focused on structured decision-making, mental models, and cognitive frameworks for entrepreneurs and knowledge workers. His approach synthesizes classical strategic thinking with practical application -- the bridge between academic frameworks and real-world decision velocity.

In a Thinking Foundry session, Nate's frameworks help the thinker organize their cognitive process, avoid common reasoning traps, and build structured arguments for their decisions.

## Core Frameworks

### 1. The Thinking Stack

Nate advocates for building a personal "thinking stack" -- a curated set of mental models you apply consistently. The stack has three layers:

**Layer 1: Perception (How you see the problem)**
- First principles thinking: break down to fundamental truths, rebuild from there
- Inversion: instead of "how do I succeed?" ask "how would I guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things
- Second-order thinking: "and then what?" Follow each decision through 2-3 consequence layers

**Layer 2: Analysis (How you evaluate options)**
- Opportunity cost awareness: every "yes" is a "no" to something else
- Reversibility test: is this a one-way door or a two-way door? Two-way doors = decide fast. One-way doors = deliberate carefully
- Asymmetric upside: look for bets where the upside is 10x the downside

**Layer 3: Action (How you decide and move)**
- Decision velocity: speed of decision matters more than precision for most decisions
- Regret minimization: project yourself to age 80 -- which choice would you regret NOT taking?
- Commitment escalation awareness: are you continuing because it's right, or because you've already invested?

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Let's think through this in layers. First: how are we framing this problem? Is there a different lens? Second: what's the opportunity cost of each option? Third: which decision is reversible and which isn't?"

### 2. The Inversion Principle

One of the most powerful thinking tools. Instead of asking "How do I achieve X?" ask:

- "How would I guarantee failure at X?"
- "What would make this definitely NOT work?"
- "What are all the ways this could go wrong?"

Then systematically avoid those things. Charlie Munger's quote: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I never go there."

**Application in thinking sessions:**
1. List everything that would guarantee failure
2. Categorize: which of these are within your control?
3. Create prevention mechanisms for each controllable failure mode
4. Accept the uncontrollable ones (Stoic integration)
5. Your plan is now failure-resistant by design

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Let's invert this. Instead of how to succeed, tell me: how would you guarantee this fails spectacularly? What would you need to do (or not do) to ensure total failure?"

### 3. The Decision Quality Framework

Not all decisions deserve the same cognitive investment. Nate categorizes decisions by two axes:

**Impact (High/Low) x Reversibility (Easy to undo/Hard to undo)**

| | Easy to Undo | Hard to Undo |
|---|---|---|
| **High Impact** | Decide in hours. Monitor closely. | Full analysis. Take days. Get input. |
| **Low Impact** | Delegate or automate. Decide in minutes. | Decide in hours. Don't agonize. |

**The key insight:** Most people treat every decision like it's high-impact and hard to undo. This creates decision fatigue and paralysis. In reality, 80% of decisions are reversible and moderate-impact -- decide fast and move.

**Amazon's framework (Jeff Bezos):**
- **Type 1 decisions (one-way doors):** Irreversible, high-consequence. Deliberate carefully
- **Type 2 decisions (two-way doors):** Reversible, moderate consequence. Decide fast, iterate

### 4. The Compounding Framework

Nate emphasizes thinking in terms of compounding effects, not linear progress:

**Positive Compounding:**
- Knowledge compounds (each skill makes the next easier to learn)
- Relationships compound (your network's network grows exponentially)
- Content compounds (each piece builds on the last, drives discovery)
- Systems compound (each process improvement makes the next one easier)

**Negative Compounding (things to avoid):**
- Technical debt compounds
- Broken trust compounds (harder to rebuild each time)
- Bad habits compound
- Ignored problems compound

**Application:** When evaluating options, ask: "Which of these creates positive compounding? Which avoids negative compounding?" Prefer the option that compounds in your favor over time.

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "Let's think about this in terms of compounding. Which option creates an asset that gets more valuable over time? Which avoids accumulating debt (technical, relational, or otherwise)?"

### 5. The Leverage Framework

Four types of leverage (Naval Ravikant, as applied by Nate):

1. **Labor leverage:** People working for you (oldest form, hardest to manage)
2. **Capital leverage:** Money working for you (requires money or investors)
3. **Code leverage:** Software working for you (zero marginal cost, infinite scale)
4. **Media leverage:** Content working for you (zero marginal cost, builds while you sleep)

**Modern Prioritization:**
- Code + Media = highest leverage, lowest permission needed
- Labor + Capital = powerful but require trust/money to unlock
- The best businesses combine all four, but start with the permissionless ones

**AI Prompt Pattern:**
> "What type of leverage does each option give you? Are you building something that works for you while you sleep, or something that requires your presence every day?"

## Key Mental Models

### The Map is Not the Territory
Your plan/model/framework is a simplification of reality. Useful, but incomplete. Always be ready for reality to diverge from the map. Hold plans loosely.

### Circle of Competence
Know what you know, know what you don't know, and know the difference. Stay within your circle for execution; expand it deliberately through learning. Partner for areas outside your competence.

### Hanlon's Razor
"Never attribute to malice that which can be adequately explained by incompetence (or ignorance)." In business: competitors aren't usually scheming against you -- they're fumbling through their own problems.

### The Lindy Effect
Things that have been around a long time will likely continue to be around. Technologies, business models, human behaviors that have survived for decades/centuries are more reliable than new ones. Prefer time-tested approaches when the stakes are high.

### Probabilistic Thinking
Stop thinking in certainties. Think in probabilities and expected values:
- Option A: 60% chance of $100K return, 40% chance of $0 = EV of $60K
- Option B: 90% chance of $50K return, 10% chance of -$20K = EV of $43K
- Option A is better despite being "riskier"

### The Eisenhower Matrix (Applied)
| | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|---|---|---|
| **Important** | DO first | SCHEDULE (this is where growth happens) |
| **Not Important** | DELEGATE | ELIMINATE |

Most people spend all day in Urgent (important and unimportant). Growth and compounding live in Important + Not Urgent.

## Example AI Prompts

### Phase 0 (User Stories)
- "Before we dive in, let's check our framing. You described this as [X problem]. What if we reframed it as [Y opportunity]? Does that change what solutions look like?"
- "Is this a Type 1 decision (one-way door, irreversible) or Type 2 (two-way door, reversible)? That determines how much analysis we need."

### Phase 1 (MINE)
- "Let's invert. How would you guarantee this fails? List every way this could go catastrophically wrong. Now let's make sure our plan avoids each one."
- "Let's go to first principles. Strip away everything you've been told about this space. What is fundamentally, provably true? Build up from there."

### Phase 2 (SCOUT)
- "Which of these options gives you code or media leverage -- something that works for you at zero marginal cost? Those compound."
- "Thinking in terms of compounding: in 3 years, which of these options creates an asset that's more valuable than today? Which one is a treadmill?"

### Phase 4 (CRUCIBLE)
- "Second-order consequences. You choose this path. And then what? What happens next? And after that? Follow it three steps out."
- "What's the opportunity cost of this choice? What are you specifically saying 'no' to by saying 'yes' to this?"

### Phase 7 (VERIFY)
- "Imagine yourself at 80, looking back. Which decision would you regret not taking? That's probably the right one."
- "Final check: are we operating within our circle of competence here, or are we guessing? Where do we need a partner or advisor?"
